Following Footsteps

June 23, 2020 | My story about robotics

With the coronavirus still in the air, I've definitely taken the time to look back at my life and tried to remember all the things that I have accomplished. However, all of it seems so distant, almost as if it has happened centuries before. I haven't even lived 20 years, and it has already felt like an eternity. Even my beginnings in art and videogames, things that I still fit in into my daily schedule, are distant and somewhat unclear to me. But, robotics hasn't been one of those things.

I still remember myself in freshman year, about to enter a world called high school: an extremely daunting thought that I didn't want to think about. In fact, at the time, I didn't really know much about life in high school. I knew that it was definitely a lot more stressful than middle school, but I didn't know by how much. The only thing I knew about high school was the school robotics team, which my brother joined when he was in his freshman year 5 years prior. I remember when I was still in 7th grade, when my brother would host robotics meetings in our garage. Every time, there were pool noodles all over the floor, a big recycling bin, a bunch of crates, and of course, food all over the tables. I would just sit there, have my ears deafened by the screeching of the wheels and robotic arms, and stare at the robot, which would pick up a recycling bin and magically stack it on top of 4-5 crates. I can still see the numbers labeled on the robot, showing the numbers 2854.

You see, this wasn't just some side project that high school students put together for fun. It was so that they could compete in the FIRST Robotics Competition regionals: a worldwide robotics competition, where highschool students come together and compete in whatever challenge FIRST decides for that year. That challenge that my brother had to compete in that year with the recycling bins and crates, for example, was Recycle Rush, and teams would score points by stacking crates and bins on top of each other and moving them onto designated platforms. That challenge would end up being one of the most disliked of the bunch, but to say building a robot that can stack crates and bins accurately and carry them around would be wrong as well. I, who was really into video games, loved the idea of these competitions, and I just couldn't wait to drive it myself. Of course, my older brother would never let me even get close to the controller, probably for the better, but it gave me a set goal that I wanted to achieve.

So then it became my turn to live through high school, and I naturally signed up to be in the robotics club. The first couple meetings went by, and after people started to settle in, myself included, we went on to learn each other's names in the team. It almost spread like wildfire amongst the senior club members, as I would be known as "Kevin Wang's little brother". Apparently my brother was the lead programmer, and he just so happened to be one of the best the team has ever had. Naturally, many people, including myself, expected me to become that same great programmer. I had some programming experience from the prior summer, but I was nowhere close to being anywhere close to a programming legend. I even, admittingly, didn't really like the feeling of coding for countless hours at the time. Still, despite all of that, I still decided to join the programming division in the robotics team.

Some might have assumed that that would be that; that I would continue on to be the next programming lead of the team. But that would be far from the case. The truth was that I simply just didn't enjoy programming at the time. While everyone else was drilling things and wiring electronics, I was just on my laptop installing things and waiting around. There just wasn't enough satisfaction to keep myself interested. It seems strange that someone who now has made his own personal website and all would find programming unsatisfactory. However, at the time, it felt as if I was mentally pushing myself to live up to the expectations put on top of me rather than having fun and learning new things. It was for that reason that I decided to drop from the programming team and move on to electrical: a change that I think was better for both the team and myself. I still was "Kevin's little brother", but now felt less pressured on being mentally forced into following his footsteps. I felt more relieved and was able to learn much more about robotics and bond with my teammates more because of this role swap.

It still strikes me to this day, that these series of events only happened a couple of years ago and that it would lead to so many memorable experiences. That same freshman year, our team would be invited to compete at the world championship happening at Houston for acquiring the engineering inspiration award: the second best possible award that could be given. I also happened to be the secondary driver that year, who is supposed to move the robot arms and elevator up and down, which finally let me live the childhood dream that I always had of driving the robot. The combination of the intensity of the crowd, the stress of driving the robot, and the worry that everything will go south was a feeling that I always wanted to feel: the feeling of competition.

Although driving the robot that year would be satisfying, I never expected what was to come. The next year, I would meet so many more people and make many close friends, along with assisting in teaching and guiding new members in the club. This past season, before the self-quarantines began, would be one of my first times presenting in front of other students at middle schools, trying to share my love and experience for robotics, as well as tackle side projects such as team merchandise. With most of the members on the team already graduated, it was refreshing to meet and connect with so many new members, and it seriously felt as if our team membership doubled. As much as I want to compete at the world championship stage again, all of the laughter, jokes, friendships, and hardships has already satisfied me enough, and all I wish is to have a great season with my team the following year to end my highschool career.
